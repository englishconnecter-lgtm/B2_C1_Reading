Fake Takeaway Apps and Free Dopamine Hits

B2 reading-comprehension lesson about symbolic experiences, digital cravings, fake shopping apps and why anticipation can feel rewarding.

Reading Comprehension

Reading: Shortened and adapted from the article text supplied for this lesson.

Approx. 520 words

In South Korea, a new kind of app has become popular among some young people: fake takeaway and fake shopping platforms that imitate the pleasure of ordering something without actually delivering anything. Users can browse pictures of food, place items in a digital basket and watch a map that appears to track a delivery. But there is no restaurant, no courier, no meal and no payment.

One example is an app called FoodNeverComes. It recreates the small rituals of ordering takeaway for people who want the feeling of treating themselves but do not want the cost, calories or inconvenience of a real order. Some users say the experience feels surprisingly satisfying because it gives them the sense that they have ordered something, even when nothing arrives.

These apps are part of what some people call “dopamine sites”. They are designed to offer quick emotional rewards through simulation rather than through the real activity. Another example is FagBreak, a site that lets users enter a virtual smoking room. They can read other people’s nicknames and messages, many of which sound like ordinary workplace complaints, without actually smoking.

For users who are studying or working alone, the fake smoking room can feel comforting because it creates the impression of taking a short break with other people. One student said that when she visits the site, she feels less lonely because other people seem to be struggling too. The site offers a social feeling without the health risks of cigarettes.

Some fake shopping apps also tell users how much money they have saved or how many calories they have avoided by not making a real purchase. Instead of rewarding spending, they reward restraint: the user receives a small emotional prize for not ordering food or not buying products. In this way, the apps turn self-control into a game.

Psychologists suggest that these platforms work because anticipation itself can be pleasurable. Looking forward to a holiday can feel almost as enjoyable as the trip, and imagining a meal can make a person hungry before the first bite. The pleasure is not only in receiving something; it can also come from expecting, imagining and preparing for it.

People have always used symbolic experiences to satisfy emotional needs. Novels transport readers to far-off places, films let viewers live other lives for a few hours, and games allow people to practise identities and choices safely. The new apps are different mainly because they imitate ordinary consumer routines, such as ordering food, shopping online or taking a break, and turn them into low-cost emotional experiences.

The trend raises questions about modern digital life. On one hand, these apps may help people avoid unnecessary spending, overeating or unhealthy habits. On the other, they show how deeply everyday apps have trained people to seek small bursts of reward from screens. The fake order may never arrive, but the feeling it creates is real.

Open comprehension questions

1. What do fake takeaway apps allow users to do?

They allow users to simulate ordering food: browsing dishes, adding items to a basket and watching a fake delivery map without paying or receiving food.

2. Why do some users find FoodNeverComes satisfying?

Because it gives them the feeling that they have ordered something, even though no real order is made.

3. How can FagBreak make lonely users feel better?

It creates the impression of taking a break with other people, so users feel less alone while studying or working.

4. How do some fake shopping apps reward users?

They show money saved or calories avoided, turning restraint into a small emotional reward.

5. What does the article suggest about anticipation?

It suggests that anticipation can be pleasurable in itself, not just the final experience.

6. Why are these apps different from older symbolic experiences like novels or films?

They imitate everyday consumer routines, such as ordering food or shopping, and turn them into low-cost emotional experiences.

Grammar

Focus: modal verbs for speculation and deduction

B2 speakers often need to discuss possible reasons for behaviour without sounding too certain. Use must, might, may, could and can’t to make careful deductions about people’s feelings, habits and motivations.

Must shows that you think something is very likely because there is strong evidence.

Might / may / could show that something is possible, but not certain.

Can’t shows that you think something is very unlikely.

Example from the lesson topic: Users might open fake takeaway apps because they want the excitement of ordering without spending money.

The comfort they feel may come from anticipation rather than from the real product.

Useful patterns

must + verb
The emotional reward must be strong if users keep returning.

might / may / could + verb
Some users might use the apps to reduce stress.

can’t + verb
The app can’t replace a real social experience, but it can imitate one.

modal + be + adjective/noun
These platforms could be a sign of changing digital habits.
