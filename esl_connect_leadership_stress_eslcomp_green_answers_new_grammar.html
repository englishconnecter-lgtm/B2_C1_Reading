Reading Comprehension

Source: Shortened and adapted from the Quartz article. Read the original article

Approx. 560 words

Leadership has always involved pressure, but the article argues that executive stress has now risen above even the peaks of the COVID-19 pandemic. This matters because stress does not stay inside the executive suite. When leaders become overwhelmed, the effects spread through the organisation.

Sustained stress narrows cognitive perspective and pushes leaders away from strategic planning into reactive thinking. In that state, they are more likely to make poor judgments, communicate badly and increase pressure on the people who work for them. The result is not only personal strain, but weaker organisational performance.

The article also explains that stress has a biological dimension. Researchers have shown that powerful stressors, especially public scrutiny, can increase cortisol, the hormone connected with the fight-or-flight response. When exposure to that state continues for too long, judgment can become distorted and impulsive reactions become more likely.

One useful idea is that leaders read crises in different ways. Some respond with calm; others respond with fast action. Some see the situation as a threat; others treat it as an opportunity. A “lighthouse” leader projects stability during turbulence, helping prevent fear from spreading through a team.

However, the article warns that no single leadership style is always correct. Calm can be valuable, but if a crisis continues, too much calm may look detached or slow. The point is not to choose one fixed identity, but to understand your default response under pressure.

The article distinguishes short, acute pressure from chronic pressure. A deadline or important presentation can sharpen focus, while months of uncertainty can damage thinking and morale. These two kinds of stress require different responses.

For that reason, the first leadership tool is self-assessment. Leaders need to know how they normally behave under pressure before they can change their response. Emotion regulation, including mindfulness, cognitive reframing and structured reflection, can create distance between a stimulus and a reaction. The aim is not to pretend stress does not exist, but to choose how to use it.

The article ends by stressing that teams learn from leaders’ behaviour. When leaders show that a crisis is survivable, people share information more freely, take more risks and think more clearly together. Managing stress is therefore not only personal resilience; it is an organisational responsibility.

Open comprehension questions

1. What has changed about executive stress?

Executive stress has risen above even the peaks of the COVID-19 pandemic.

2. How does sustained stress affect leaders’ thinking?

It narrows cognitive perspective and pushes leaders from strategic planning into reactive thinking.

3. Why does the article mention cortisol?

Because prolonged fight-or-flight responses can distort judgment and increase impulsive reactions.

4. What is the difference between acute and chronic pressure?

Short, acute pressure can sharpen performance, while chronic pressure across months can damage thinking and morale.

5. What is emotion regulation supposed to help leaders do?

It helps leaders create distance between a stressful stimulus and their response, so they can choose how to act.

6. Why does a leader’s stress response matter for the whole team?

Because teams take cues from leaders; when leaders show a crisis is survivable, people communicate and think more clearly.

Grammar

Focus: nominalisation for advanced leadership analysis

In business and academic writing, writers often turn actions into noun phrases. This makes ideas sound more concise, formal and analytical.

Instead of writing leaders regulate emotions, we can write emotion regulation. Instead of stress narrows thinking, we can write the narrowing of cognitive perspective.

Key grammar

Nominalisation means changing a verb or adjective into a noun or noun phrase.

Verb → noun: regulate → regulation; assess → assessment; expose → exposure.

Clause → noun phrase: when leaders invite disagreement → the invitation of disagreement.

Useful patterns

The regulation of + noun
The regulation of emotional responses is essential under pressure.

Exposure to + noun phrase
Exposure to public scrutiny can intensify stress.

The + noun + of + noun phrase
The narrowing of perspective can lead to poor decisions.

Constructive + abstract noun
Constructive disagreement helps leaders avoid reactive thinking.
