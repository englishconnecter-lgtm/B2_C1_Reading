Before you read

This lesson explores whether family businesses still offer the same route to upward mobility that they once did. The article compares century-old companies, rising costs and the changing meaning of the American Dream.

Reading Comprehension

Source: Shortened and adapted from the CNBC Make It article pasted into the lesson prompt.

Approx. 520 words

Some family businesses in the United States have survived for more than a century, but their owners say entrepreneurship no longer guarantees the same upward mobility it once did. The article focuses on long-running businesses whose founders once used business ownership as a path to independence and stability.

Starr Hutchings Purdue co-runs Hutchings Funeral Home in Macon, Georgia, which her great-grandfather opened in 1910. For earlier generations, owning the business brought freedom and enough financial success to educate the family. Today, however, the funeral industry is more competitive and expensive, so the business no longer creates the same level of upward mobility.

The article argues that this change is not limited to one company. Entrepreneurship has long been seen as a classic route to the American Dream, yet many present-day small-business owners struggle to become better off than their parents. Some still own homes, cars and respected companies, but their money does not stretch as far as it once did.

M&S Schmalberg, a 110-year-old fabric-flower business in New York, shows this contradiction clearly. The company is profitable and its flowers appear in fashion and television, yet co-owner Adam Brand says his house is smaller than the one he grew up in. Factory rent has also risen sharply, making business success feel less financially transformative.

Ric Serrano’s family business in Arizona also shows how older companies must adapt. Serrano’s began as a dry-goods store in 1919, became a department store and later turned into a Mexican restaurant chain. At its peak it had eight locations, but now it has two. Serrano says higher wages, rising costs and customers cutting back on dining out have made growth extremely difficult.

Not every story in the article is negative. David Judson, who runs a Los Angeles stained-glass studio founded by his family, says he is building rather than merely keeping up. By moving into contemporary art and public projects, he has found new sources of revenue and kept control over the business.

The article ends by suggesting that the American Dream is not only about money. For some owners, preserving a family legacy, controlling their own work and having flexibility still matter deeply. But if small businesses no longer provide broad social mobility, the shared cultural promise of building a better future may weaken.

In this context, hopefulness means the belief that people can build a better future for themselves and their children, even when the path to upward mobility has become more difficult.

Grammar

Focus: concession and contrast in complex arguments

Inspired by the source: The article does not simply say family businesses are failing. It shows contrast: some companies are profitable, yet owners still feel less upwardly mobile than previous generations.

Key grammar

Concession clauses introduce an idea that contrasts with the main point: although, even though, while, whereas, despite the fact that.

Advanced contrast helps writers build nuanced arguments instead of one-sided claims.

Useful patterns

Although / Even though + clause, main clause
Although the business is profitable, the owner feels less financially secure than previous generations.

Main clause; however, contrast clause
The company has survived for a century; however, modern costs are limiting its growth.

Whereas + clause, contrasting clause
Whereas entrepreneurship once promised mobility, today it may simply preserve stability.

Despite + noun phrase, main clause
Despite rising costs, many owners still value independence.

Useful language

Evaluating change: The main shift seems to be... / Compared with previous generations...

Balancing arguments: On the one hand... / At the same time...

Adding nuance: It is not simply a question of... / The reality is more complex because...

Referring to evidence: The example of... shows that...
