Reading Comprehension

Source article: CNBC article on answering a difficult interview question

In a competitive job market, interviewers are not only checking a candidate’s technical experience. They are also listening for signs of a positive, collaborative attitude. Career expert Erin McGoff argues that interpersonal skills have become especially important because, as artificial intelligence takes over more technical tasks, the ability to work with people remains difficult to replace.

One common behavioral question is: Tell me about a time you disagreed with your boss or co-worker. The question may sound uncomfortable, but it gives candidates a chance to show maturity, judgement and emotional intelligence. The goal is not to prove that a former manager was wrong. The goal is to show that you can handle workplace conflict professionally and constructively.

McGoff’s first recommendation is to keep the example professional, not personal. A candidate should avoid choosing a story about personality clashes, office gossip or a refused holiday request. Instead, the disagreement should be connected to the business: a project, a client, a timeline, a strategy or a different interpretation of evidence.

It is also useful to frame the situation as a difference in opinion rather than an argument. For example, a candidate might begin by saying that they have worked with many strong managers, but there have been moments when they had to advocate for an alternative perspective. This opening sounds balanced because it avoids blaming another person and focuses on the work.

After introducing the example, McGoff recommends using the STAR method: situation, task, action and result. First, explain the context and your responsibility. For instance, you might say that your team was preparing a project for a client and that the original plan was moving in a particular direction. However, you had information suggesting that another approach might better serve the client’s needs.

Next, describe the action you took. A strong answer shows that you communicated calmly, asked for a one-to-one conversation and explained your reasoning clearly. You might say that you presented an alternative path, supported it with evidence and made the case for why it would be better for the client.

Finally, describe the result. A good answer might explain that you and your boss compromised, adjusted the plan and achieved a successful outcome. The result should show cooperation, not victory over the other person. You can also mention what you learned from the experience, such as how to raise concerns earlier or how to listen more carefully to another point of view.

The strongest answers show “healthy conflict”: disagreement that helps people make better decisions. In the workplace, being able to disagree respectfully is not a weakness. It is evidence that you can communicate under pressure, protect relationships and focus on the shared goal.

Exercise 1: Open comprehension questions

1. Why are interpersonal skills especially important in today’s job market?

Because AI may take over some technical skills, while human communication and collaboration remain difficult to replace.

2. What should a candidate show when answering this question?

They should show that they can handle workplace conflict professionally and constructively.

3. Why should the conflict be professional rather than personal?

A professional example sounds mature and focuses on business goals rather than personality clashes.

4. What action should the candidate describe?

They should describe calmly communicating their opinion, asking for a one-to-one conversation and explaining their reasoning.

5. What should the result of the story show?

It should show cooperation, compromise and a positive outcome, not that the candidate defeated the boss.

Grammar

Advanced grammar theme: participle clauses for concise interview answers

In interviews, candidates often need to explain a situation clearly without sounding too long. Participle clauses help connect actions, reasons and results in a concise professional style.

Useful patterns: Having + past participle, main clause. / Past participle phrase, main clause. / Verb-ing phrase, main clause.
