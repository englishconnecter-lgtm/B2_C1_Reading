Reading Comprehension

Source: Shortened and adapted from CNBC Make It. Read the original article

Busy senior people often receive more messages than they can properly read. An email that does not make its purpose clear may be postponed, forgotten or deleted. This is why the final line of a professional email is so important. It is not just a polite ending; it tells the reader exactly what action is needed next.

Many professionals end messages with general phrases such as “let me know your thoughts” or “feel free to reach out.” These expressions sound friendly, but they create extra work for the receiver. The person has to decide whether the writer wants approval, feedback, a meeting, a decision or simply acknowledgement. When the reader has to interpret the request, the chance of getting no reply increases.

The article argues that a stronger email closing should reduce effort for the reader. One useful strategy is to ask a binary question. Instead of asking for general thoughts, the writer offers two clear choices, such as two possible meeting days or two possible options. A simple either-or question turns an open request into a quick decision.

Another strategy is to attach a clear deadline to the request. A message such as “Could you reply by Thursday so I can move forward?” explains both the timing and the reason. This makes the request feel more purposeful and helps the reader understand the consequence of not replying. A deadline is most effective when it is connected to a real next step, not used as pressure for its own sake.

The article also recommends making the response extremely easy. A senior leader may be checking emails between meetings, so a request that can be answered with one word is more likely to receive attention. For example, the writer might ask the person to reply “yes” if they agree, or to send one preferred date so that the writer can arrange the next step.

The wider message is that strong communication is not about sounding complicated. It is about being specific, respectful and easy to respond to. Clear closing lines show confidence because they guide the conversation forward. They also help the sender avoid wasting time waiting for a vague response that may never come. In professional contexts, clarity can be a sign of respect for another person’s time.

Grammar

Focus: indirect questions and noun clauses in professional requests

Inspired by the source: The article argues that professionals should avoid vague closings and ask for specific action. This grammar focus helps students make requests sound precise, polite and professional.

Basic idea: In formal emails, we often turn a direct question into an indirect question or noun clause.

Direct: Do you prefer option A or option B?
Indirect: Could you confirm whether you prefer option A or option B?

Useful patterns

Could you confirm whether + clause?
Could you confirm whether you approve the proposal?

Please let me know which + noun + clause.
Please let me know which option you prefer.

Could you tell me when/why/how + clause?
Could you tell me when you would like to meet?

I want to clarify what + clause.
I want to clarify what action you need from me next.

What I need is...
What I need is your approval by Friday.

Useful language

Making a request: Could you confirm whether...?

Giving a reason: This would help me to...

Offering options: Would A or B work better?

Following up: I just wanted to check whether...
