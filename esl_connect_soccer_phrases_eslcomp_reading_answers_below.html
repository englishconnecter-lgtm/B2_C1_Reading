Soccer language & tactics

Article: The Conversation — What do soccer’s strange phrases mean?

Classroom lesson based on a shortened, adapted version of the article.

Reading Comprehension

Source: Shortened and adapted from The Conversation article. Read the original article

Approx. 560 words

Every four years, the World Cup attracts both passionate supporters and casual viewers. Hardcore fans may follow possession percentages, expected goals, duels won and corners, but newer viewers can find the language of the game confusing. Soccer has developed a distinctive vocabulary that helps fans, coaches and players describe what is happening on the pitch.

Some phrases describe a single player’s technique, while others explain a team’s overall strategy. They can be technical, informal or highly visual. One famous example is “park the bus”, often linked to José Mourinho. The phrase describes a team defending extremely deep, as if it had placed a bus in front of the goal to block all attacking space.

Another common phrase is “build-up play”. This means a structured sequence of passes that moves the ball from the defensive third of the field into attacking areas. Teams that are patient in possession often rely on build-up play rather than simply kicking the ball forward.

The article also explains how the pitch can be imagined as different vertical lanes. A central corridor runs through the middle, while two wing corridors run down the sides. If a player moves from the wing into the centre, they may help create an “overload”, meaning their team has more players in that area than the opposition can easily defend.

A “false nine” is a forward who drops back into midfield rather than staying close to the goal. This can pull a defender out of position and open space for wingers to attack. A related tactical idea is “counter-pressing”: when a team loses the ball, it immediately tries to win it back instead of retreating into a defensive shape.

Defensive phrases can also be confusing. In zonal marking, players protect spaces rather than mark individual opponents. In an offside trap, defenders step forward together just before a pass is played, hoping an attacker will be caught offside. A coach may shout “step up” or “push up” to tell the defensive line to move forward.

Some phrases are simple instructions. “Switch the play” means passing the ball across the field to the less defended side. “Drop off” tells a defender to retreat, while “man on” warns a player that an opponent is close and may tackle them.

Other expressions are connected to soccer history. “Total football” became famous through Dutch coaches Rinus Michels and Johan Cruyff, and describes a system where outfield players can swap roles instead of staying fixed in one position. “Panenka” describes a risky penalty in which the kicker softly chips the ball over a diving goalkeeper. Together, these phrases form a shared language for billions of fans around the world.

Grammar

Focus: defining and explaining technical terms

Inspired by the source: The article explains specialist soccer phrases for readers who may not know the sport well. At B2/C1 level, clear definitions often use relative clauses, reduced relative clauses and explanatory phrases.

Useful structures

Term + refers to + noun phrase
“Total football” refers to a system in which players can swap roles.

A phrase used to describe...
“Park the bus” is a phrase used to describe very deep defending.

...in which...
Counter-pressing is a strategy in which a team tries to win the ball back immediately.
